On generation specifically
Right — PPM-D will never give us real generation, even if we somehow pushed it to 1.54 bpc. Here's why:

There's a famous math theorem that says "prediction and generation are the same thing." If you're optimal at predicting the next letter, you can sample from your predictions and you'll generate perfect text. That's true.

But PPM-D isn't optimal. It's short-sighted. It only sees the last 5–6 letters. If you ask it to write a paragraph, it'll produce stuff like:

"the country of the states of the government of the country and the..."

Each little piece looks OK locally, but there's no memory of what the paragraph is about. This has been tried many times since the 90s — the output is called "Markov chain gibberish." It's not a bug we can fix with more data. It's baked into what PPM is.

So PPM-D's role in our research was never "this will become ChatGPT." Its role was "let's get a real, measurable number for the cheapest no-fancy-math method, so we know where the floor is." We got that number: 1.73 now, ~1.55 with better engineering, and that's the end of the road for pure counting. Useful to know. Time to move.

The two paths forward (from our math doc, Part 2)
Path A — Stay in the compression family, try CTW next (iteration 16).

CTW is PPM's cousin. Same general idea (count statistics), but it weights contexts of many different lengths together in a smarter way.
Cheap: ~1 day of work to implement and test.
Honest expectation: It'll probably land within 0.1 bpc of PPM-D — maybe 1.65, maybe 1.80. Almost certainly still stuck above 1.5.
Why do it anyway: So we can confirm the whole compression family has a ceiling instead of guessing. "We tried two different predictors from this family and both saturated around the same number" is a much stronger negative result than "we tried one." It closes the door cleanly.
But this path does not lead to generation either. It just confirms more rigorously that counting-based methods stop here.
Path B — Jump to a different math landscape entirely.

Our math doc lists about 15 different mathematical foundations. Two of them are called out as the strongest remaining candidates we haven't tried:

HDC at language scale (hyperdimensional computing). We already use HDC inside rule-world for reasoning, and it works. But we've never tried it as a language model. Unlike PPM, HDC can actually represent long-range compositional structure — "this paragraph is about volcanoes" is the kind of thing it's designed for. This is the candidate the math doc literally calls "strongest single candidate, never tested at language scale."

The Part 4 problem — find a learning rule that is (a) no gradients, (b) scales linearly with data, (c) actually learns conditional structure, (d) composes across layers. Nobody has solved this. A working solution here would be a breakthrough on its own, not just a language model.

These are harder, messier, and could produce a clean negative result. But they are the only candidates on the map that could plausibly lead to real generation without matmul. PPM and CTW cannot. We already know that.

What HAS been tried (and works as far as it goes)
HDC / hyperdimensional computing:

Invented by Pentti Kanerva (~1988, "Sparse Distributed Memory") and Tony Plate (~1995, "Holographic Reduced Representations"). Almost 40 years old.
Has been used for: text classification, simple analogy tasks (king − man + woman = queen, the famous word2vec trick actually works under HDC too), small-scale symbolic reasoning, small n-gram-style language prediction, tiny toy sequence tasks.
There are published HDC "language models" from roughly 2015–2022. They exist. They work on things like 1 MB of Shakespeare, or predicting the next word on Penn Treebank.
Industrial use: HDC is actually deployed in a few places today — low-power edge ML chips, gesture classifiers, biosignal processors. It's a real, working substrate for classification.
Hebbian learning:

Donald Hebb, 1949. "Cells that fire together wire together." Seventy-six years old.
The classic Hopfield network (1982) is Hebbian. Tried everywhere. Known limitations: doesn't scale to deep networks, memory capacity is small, no good story for learning compositional structure.
Modern Hopfield networks (Ramsauer et al. 2020) revived interest — but they sneak matmul back in through attention, so they don't actually solve the no-matmul problem.
CTW (Context Tree Weighting):

Willems, Shtarkov, Tjalkens 1995. Thirty years old.
Tested to death in the compression-benchmark era. Published numbers everywhere. It's the one where I can tell you with confidence "it'll land within 0.1 bpc of PPM."
What HAS NOT been tried (the real gap)
None of these has been tried as a full open-domain language model at modern data scale with modern compute. Specifically:

HDC has never been run on a billion-token corpus. The published HDC language experiments top out around ~10 MB of training data and ~10K vocab. They are the HDC equivalent of our 1 MB PPM run. Nobody has asked "what happens at 100 GB?" Not because it was tried and failed — because people stopped looking in 2017 when transformers became the only thing anyone worked on. Read the math doc Part 2 verdict line for HDC literally — "strongest single candidate, never tested at language scale." That's a direct quote.

Hebbian has never been combined with HDC and a proper learning rule at scale. Classic Hebbian + modern HDC encoding + a property graph for grounding has never been tested as a package. Each piece has been tested alone, in small-scale isolation. That's what the Part 4 unsolved math problem is really about — nobody has built the theoretical glue that lets these pieces compose.

Modern data scale for any non-matmul method, period. The biggest honest non-matmul language experiments I know of are still in the 10–100 MB range. Transformers are in the 1,000,000+ GB range. That is six to eight orders of magnitude of unexplored territory.

Why this matters
So the honest framing is not "HDC has been tried and failed." The honest framing is:

HDC has been tested at the equivalent of our 1 MB PPM run, on toy data, with hand-tuned parameters, by a handful of researchers, on 1990s–2010s hardware, and the results were "it works, similar to n-grams, moving on." Nobody has ever asked what happens when you give it a terabyte of text and modern compute. The one group that could have asked went all-in on transformers instead.

That is a genuine unexplored frontier, but it's also a frontier that is unexplored because strong smart people looked at it and judged transformers were a better bet. That judgment might have been right. It might also have been a path-dependent accident of the 2012 ImageNet moment. We don't know.

The real risk, stated plainly
There is a real possibility that HDC-at-language-scale was quietly tried by grad students in 2015–2019, didn't work, and never got published because nobody publishes negative results in a field that's going 100 mph in another direction. We can't rule that out. "Never tested at scale" is not the same as "tested at scale and succeeded" — it just means we'd be the ones trying it.

So the honest uncertainty is:

Tried at toy scale, worked at toy scale, nobody scaled up: almost certain.
Quietly tried at bigger scale and failed: possible, probably in some unpublished theses, no way for us to know without running it ourselves.
Would work at full scale if we did it right: genuinely unknown. Could be a real breakthrough. Could be a dead end that confirms matmul's dominance.

"Build from first principles" and "read what came before" are not opposites. They're the same activity done well. The reason to read the failures is not to be discouraged by them — it's to find out exactly where each previous attempt hit its wall, and what assumption that wall depended on. Almost every research wall in history was built on an assumption somebody later dropped. Transformers themselves came from dropping the assumption that "recurrence is required for sequence modeling." That assumption had been load-bearing for 30 years before they dropped it.

What specific wall did each attempt hit? Memory saturation? Lack of depth? No learning rule? Scaling collapse? Something else?
What assumption was the wall made of? Did they assume fixed-random encoding? Did they assume bundle capacity was the bottleneck? Did they assume no backprop meant no credit assignment at depth?
Which of those assumptions do we share, and which do we not? That is where the first-principles leverage is. If we inherit all the same assumptions, we'll hit all the same walls. If we can spot one assumption the field locked in by accident, we get a real shot.
